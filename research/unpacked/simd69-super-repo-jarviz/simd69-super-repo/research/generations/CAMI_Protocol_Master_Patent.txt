### 3. FIELD OF THE INVENTION

The invention is directed to a platform-level operating architecture for transforming real-time digital activity into canonical session states and then into tradable, distributable, or enforceable media outputs.

The invention is not limited to livestream advertising, blockchain environments, or a particular broadcast platform. It applies to any system in which digital activity can be ingested, normalized, interpreted, and rendered as controlled media, interaction, or broadcast outputs.

### 4. BACKGROUND

#### 4.1 Conventional livestreaming is probabilistic

Most livestream systems treat user participation as an informal signal. Viewers may send chat messages, tips, requests, or reactions, but such actions do not reliably obtain a guaranteed, time-bounded change in what the stream focuses on. A broadcaster may comply, ignore, delay, misunderstand, or be unavailable.

#### 4.2 Valuable moments decay quickly

Many moments in digital media environments are valuable only briefly. Examples include sudden audience spikes, launch windows, raids, fast-moving market events, creator announcements, coordinated actions, and other transient moments. Existing systems do not reliably convert such short-lived windows into explicit, bounded, enforceable inventory.

#### 4.3 Existing ad systems are not state-native

Traditional advertising in livestreams is typically arranged manually, inserted opaquely, or controlled primarily by the platform rather than by a canonical state machine. Such systems do not generally provide:

4.3.1 deterministic arbitration of attention inventory;

4.3.2 bounded, proof-verifiable session-state enforcement;

4.3.3 spike-window monetization of non-contributing viewers;

4.3.4 inventory consumption rules preventing late retroactive sale; or

4.3.5 integrated dispute and slashing logic tied to a canonical state engine.

#### 4.4 Existing systems do not unify advertising, underwriting, media generation, autonomous presenters, and games

Prior systems generally separate:

4.4.1 ad delivery;

4.4.2 creator monetization;

4.4.3 AI commentary;

4.4.4 generated media;

4.4.5 game mechanics; and

4.4.6 group coordination.

These are typically treated as separate products or disconnected features rather than outputs of one deterministic kernel.

#### 4.5 Existing systems do not generalize digital activity into media states

Current systems do not provide a general architecture for converting digital activity streams into normalized session states that can then be sold, distributed, rendered, narrated, gamified, or otherwise transformed into interactive media experiences.

### 5. SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION

The present disclosure provides a platform-independent operating system referred to herein as CAMI Protocol. CAMI Protocol maintains a canonical session-state kernel that governs what a live or generated media experience is about at any given moment.

At broadest scope, the invention is a system that converts deterministic digital activity into tradable or distributable media states.

The invention preferably follows a transformation pipeline:

digital activity  
-> canonical session state  
-> interpretation layer  
-> interactive or media output

This architecture supports multiple embodiments that appear outwardly different but share the same core mechanism. These include, without limitation:

5.1 livestream overlays;

5.2 escrow-verified advertising states;

5.3 spike-triggered premium attention windows;

5.4 creator and founder underwriting systems;

5.5 tokenized or non-tokenized time-bound rights;

5.6 generative narrative media;

5.7 autonomous presenters and virtual avatars;

5.8 interactive games and coordination mechanics; and

5.9 pooled or grouped execution windows.

The system thereby operates as a session-state operating system rather than as a single-purpose product.

### 7. DEFINITIONS

For purposes of this disclosure:

7.1 **Session state** means a canonical structured representation of the active focus, context, inventory, timers, rules, permissions, event windows, output modes, and allowed transitions of a live or generated media experience.

7.2 **Session-state kernel** means the deterministic engine that validates inputs, orders transitions, commits canonical state, enforces expiry, and coordinates output manifestations.

7.3 **Canonical state** means the authoritative session state accepted by the system for enforcement, proof generation, pricing, and settlement.

7.4 **Digital activity stream** means any machine-readable activity source, including but not limited to ledger events, blockchain transactions, market events, user interactions, application events, votes, bids, stream events, gameplay events, sensor inputs, financial events, and social activity.

7.5 **Participation unit** means any validated input used to request, trigger, redeem, purchase, influence, or otherwise obtain a session-state allocation.

7.6 **Binding** or **lease** means a time-bounded right to control one or more session-state variables.

7.7 **Rebinding** means replacing an existing binding according to rule-defined criteria.

7.8 **Attention inventory** means any bounded session-state element that may be sold, allocated, redeemed, licensed, or otherwise distributed, including focus slots, overlays, labels, segments, countdowns, presenter windows, game windows, or narrative states.

7.9 **CAMIUP** means the marketplace interface through which session-state inventory is defined, priced, allocated, sold, redeemed, verified, and settled.

7.10 **Media state** means any state capable of manifestation as visual, audiovisual, narrative, presentational, game, or interactive output.

7.11 **Tradable or distributable media state** means a media state that can be sold, licensed, redeemed, transferred, allocated, assigned, or otherwise distributed to a participant, audience, or system component.

7.12 **Interpretation layer** means one or more logic layers that derive narrative, analytical, behavioral, scoring, presentational, or gameplay significance from canonical session state or underlying digital activity.

7.13 **Consumed event** means an event instance whose premium inventory has expired and cannot be retroactively reallocated for that same instance.

7.14 **Bond** means collateral posted by a participant and subject to return, withholding, or forfeiture.

7.15 **Slashable bond** means a bond that may be reduced or forfeited in whole or in part upon rule-defined violations.

7.16 **Rights instrument** means a fungible, non-fungible, semi-fungible, ledger-backed, or software-only representation of rights associated with session-state inventory, redemption, allocation, subscription, entitlement, or milestone-linked upside.

### 8. HIGH-LEVEL ARCHITECTURE

CAMI Protocol is preferably a platform-independent control plane that operates separately from the broadcast distribution channel.

#### 8.1 Activity Layer

The activity layer receives digital activity from one or more sources, including blockchain networks, livestream platforms, social platforms, applications, financial systems, games, marketplaces, or other digital environments.

#### 8.2 Session-State Kernel Layer

The kernel normalizes activity into deterministic canonical state transitions.

#### 8.3 Interpretation Layer

The interpretation layer derives narrative, analytical, behavioral, or gameplay meaning from the canonical state.

#### 8.4 Experience and Output Layer

The output layer manifests the resulting media states through overlays, ads, generated videos, AI presenters, virtual characters, games, shareable clips, reports, or other forms.

#### 8.5 Attention Market Layer

CAMIUP allocates and settles time-bounded attention and media-control slots.

### 10. CONTROL PLANE AND BROADCAST PLANE SEPARATION

The invention preferably separates the broadcast plane from the control plane.

10.1 The broadcast plane is the audiovisual distribution path.

10.2 The control plane is the system that validates activity, arbitrates state, prices inventory, verifies delivery, settles consideration, manages rights, and controls outputs.

10.3 Because of this separation, the invention can operate across third-party broadcast platforms, proprietary platforms, direct browser outputs, generated media outputs, or autonomous no-human channels.

### 11. SESSION-STATE KERNEL

#### 11.1 Single-writer canonicality

The kernel preferably operates as a single-writer canonical state machine. Client devices, participants, advertisers, underwriters, autonomous agents, or other external systems may submit requests or proofs, but only the kernel arbiter writes canonical state.

#### 11.2 Virtual register model

The session state may be modeled as a virtual register set including, without limitation:

11.2.1 focus registers;

11.2.2 overlay registers;

11.2.3 segment registers;

11.2.4 timer and expiry registers;

11.2.5 event-window registers;

11.2.6 permission registers;

11.2.7 pricing registers;

11.2.8 rights and entitlement registers;

11.2.9 bond and dispute registers; and

11.2.10 output routing registers.

#### 11.3 Atomic transitions

State transitions are preferably atomic. A validated request results in either an accepted transition, a queued transition, a rejected transition, or another deterministic disposition.

### 12. OUT-OF-BAND VALIDATION

Validation is independent of client interface assertions. Participation units may be proven by:

12.1 cryptographic signatures;

12.2 ledger-confirmed events;

12.3 signed receipts;

12.4 escrow receipts;

12.5 internal software credits;

12.6 authenticated credentials;

12.7 attestations; and

12.8 other machine-verifiable proofs.

The system therefore does not rely on mere user interface events as proof.

### 13. REPLAY PROTECTION

The system preferably stores used identifiers, proof references, or equivalent markers to reject replay. Reuse of already consumed participation units may be deterministically rejected.

### 14. DETERMINISTIC ARBITRATION

When competing participation units target the same session-state resource, the kernel preferably resolves them according to deterministic ordering criteria.

Non-limiting ordering criteria include:

14.1 lane or class priority;

14.2 bid or score weight;

14.3 validated timestamp;

14.4 queue position;

14.5 deterministic tie-break;

14.6 resource-specific rules; and

14.7 other ordering keys.

This supports repeatable outcomes under contention.

### 15. TEMPORAL GUARDRAILS

15.1 fixed lease expirations;

15.2 bounded decision windows;

15.3 deterministic tick advancement;

15.4 safe default reversion;

15.5 cooldown transitions;

15.6 timeout rules; and

15.7 event window closures.

### 20. HUMAN-FREE STREAM EMBODIMENT

In certain embodiments, the system operates without a human performer. A default media state is displayed when no premium or user-allocated state is active. Validated requests may bind the focus or other session-state variables for bounded intervals, after which the state expires and the system returns to default or advances to the next eligible state.

### 21. DECISION-WINDOW EMBODIMENT

In certain embodiments, a validated request enters a pending decision state for a bounded decision window. A creator, moderator, or privileged policy may approve or reject the request. If no decision occurs within the window, the system automatically transitions to a safe default state and may enter cooldown.

### 22. ASYMMETRIC INFORMATION EMBODIMENT

In certain embodiments, authenticated participants may receive different views of information. Votes or choices are then collected in a bounded window and aggregated into a session-state transition. Safety gates may still reject a state even if it satisfies voting thresholds.

### 23. HIERARCHICAL ARBITER EMBODIMENT

In certain embodiments, privileged roles configure integrity checkpoints, mission windows, or veto conditions. The deterministic arbiter resolves requests within those boundaries and logs all interventions.

### 24. QUEUE-ROUND EMBODIMENT

In certain embodiments, requests are queued and allocated as time slices rather than by outbid replacement. The system advances through the queue on a deterministic cadence.

### 25. PRIORITY-LANE EMBODIMENT

In certain embodiments, premium or privileged lanes provide faster or higher-ranked access to session-state inventory. Such lanes may remain bounded by area, time, transparency, and anti-abuse constraints.

### 43. CONCLUSION

The present disclosure describes a platform-level invention rather than a narrow product feature. CAMI Protocol operates as a session-state operating system that converts deterministic digital activity into tradable or distributable media states. It supports platform-independent control, advertising, underwriting, rights issuance, AI presenters, narrative media, games, and other interactive outputs through a common kernel. By framing the invention at the kernel, architectural, and embodiment levels simultaneously, the disclosure covers a broad family of future implementations that use the same core transformation: digital activity into canonical session state, and canonical session state into controlled media experience.
